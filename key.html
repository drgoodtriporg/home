Phase 1: Screening & Assessment

Goal: To determine if psychedelic-assisted therapy is appropriate and safe for the patient's specific physiological and psychological history .

Comprehensive Review

  • Holistic Screening: Includes physiological and psychological assessments to rule out conditions that complicate treatment .
  • Baselines: Use validated tools (e.g., CAPS-5 for PTSD, MADRS for depression) to establish a baseline and track symptoms .
  • Medical History: Screen for cardiac conditions, which are primary physical contraindications .
  • Medications: Assess for drug interactions; patients may need to discontinue antidepressants 6–8 weeks prior to the session .

Trauma & Exclusions

  • Trauma Assessment: Identify trauma history to understand what may arise, but avoid detailed descriptions to prevent premature triggering .
  • Contraindications: Common exclusions include psychosis, schizophrenia, bipolar disorder (compound dependent), and cardiovascular disease .

Phase 2: Preparation

Goal: To build trust, educate on the philosophy, and establish safety protocols over roughly three 90-minute sessions .

The Therapeutic Alliance

  • Rapport is paramount; a lack of trust can lead to overwhelming experiences .
  • Patients are encouraged to consciously "choose to trust" and voice any ruptures immediately .
The Acceptance Model

Inner Healing Intelligence: Patients are taught they have an innate capacity to heal their own wounds; the therapy relies on the patient discovering their own answers .

Key Mantras: To aid surrender, therapists teach mantras like: "Trust, let go, be open" or "Enter into it and look out through its eyes" .

Counter Avoidance: Patients must be prepared to face experiences head-on rather than intellectualizing or avoiding them .

Logistics & Consent

  • Informed Consent: An ongoing conversation, not just a form. Patients can revoke consent at any time .
  • Touch Boundaries: Explicitly agree on supportive touch (e.g., hand-holding) before the session starts .
  • Physical Prep: Advice on fasting/light breakfast and comfortable clothing .

Phase 3: Set and Setting

Goal: To optimize the patient's mindset (Set) and the physical environment (Setting) .

Set (Mindset)

  • Includes expectations, motivations, and mood .
  • Ensure the patient is calm; if acutely distressed, the session should be postponed .

Setting (Environment)

  • Physical Space: A living-room-like environment with soft lighting and couches is preferred over sterile rooms .
  • Personalization: Patients may bring meaningful items (photos, blankets) for safety .
  • Sensory Tools: Eyeshades and headphones with music facilitate inward focus .

Phase 4: The Medication Session

Goal: A multi-hour appointment (often 6–8 hours) where the therapist supports the patient's inward journey .

Acceptance in Action

Surrender: Patients are instructed to "accept whatever comes up," believing it is exactly what is supposed to happen .

The Only Way Out Is Through: If shame, guilt, or fear arise, patients are encouraged to stay with the feelings fully rather than resisting .

Therapist Role: Nondirective

  • Midwifery Approach: Provide safety and reassurance but do not guide or direct the journey .
  • Silence: Therapists remain largely silent unless engaged by the patient .
  • Non-Judgment: "Turn off the rational mind" and avoid pathologizing the experience .

Handling Challenges

  • Provide grounding presence or agreed-upon touch for anxiety .
  • Monitor physiological safety (BP, heart rate) at scheduled intervals .

Phase 5: Integration

Goal: To assimilate insights from the session into daily life, beginning the morning after the session .

Key Activities

  • Processing: Journaling and detailed recall to capture salient aspects of the experience .
  • Meaning-Making: Apply insights specifically to the diagnosis (e.g., depression, PTSD) .
  • Frameworks: Therapists may use Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) or Internal Family Systems (IFS) to structure integration .

Navigating Changes

  • Ontological Shock: Be prepared for profound shifts in worldview or sense of self (ego dissolution) .
  • Life Decisions: Advise patients to avoid major life decisions immediately after the session .

Safety & Ethical Practice

Overview: Safety is foundational. Precautions must be taken regarding psychological, physiological, and ethical risks .

Psychological & Physiological

  • Suicidality: Watch for suicidality, especially during early integration phases .
  • Spiritual Emergency: Be aware that some experiences may resemble psychosis but are part of the process .
  • Vitals: Monitor for elevated heart rate and blood pressure .

Ethical Conduct

  • Vulnerability: Patients are in a highly vulnerable state; strict boundaries prevent sexual or financial abuse .
  • Protection: Measures such as having two therapists present and video recording are recommended .
